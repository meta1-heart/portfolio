The Project

The CUBE is a multiplayer PvPvE extraction shooter set in the Atomic Heart universe. The game world takes the form of a Rubik's Cube ‚Äî each of its 6 faces is a distinct biome, and each biome is divided into a 3√ó3 grid of levels, with each level measuring 400√ó400 meters. I joined during pre-production as the Senior Level Designer on a team of 6. My primary responsibility wasn't building individual levels ‚Äî it was figuring out how we build levels at all at this scale, and making sure the team could execute efficiently once production began.

The Challenge

Pre-production on a project of this scale posed a fundamental question: how do you prototype 54 large-scale levels fast enough to validate gameplay before committing to full art production?

Core Challenges

The Rubik's Cube structure meant each level needed to work both standalone (as an extraction zone) and as part of a larger biome grid (connected to adjacent levels). The Atomic Heart universe demanded a specific visual identity per biome. The team included junior designers who needed to be productive quickly. And the sheer square footage ‚Äî over 8 km¬≤ total ‚Äî meant traditional hand-crafting of every space would take years.

My job was to solve these problems at the systems level ‚Äî not by designing faster, but by building tools and processes that let the whole team design faster.

The kit consisted of ~25‚Äì30 snap-together modules based on a 4√ó4 meter grid ‚Äî walls, corners, doorways, windows, floor sections ‚Äî that could be combined to create buildings of any size and layout in minutes instead of days.

Design Decision: 4m Grid

We chose a 4√ó4 meter module size optimized for gameplay rather than architectural realism. The grid needed to accommodate player movement, combat ranges, and cover placement. Realistic room proportions would have created spaces that felt wrong to play in ‚Äî so we prioritized how spaces feel in gameplay over how they'd look in real life.

Impact

A building that previously took 3‚Äì4 days to greybox could now be assembled in under a day. Junior designers could pick up the kit and start building immediately ‚Äî the snap system and standardized metrics eliminated guesswork and enforced consistent quality across the team.

04

Cross-Department Collaboration

The Rubik's Cube world structure required constant alignment between level design, engineering, and art. Each department had constraints that affected the others, and pre-production was where these constraints needed to be discovered and resolved.

With Engineering

The cube's face-and-grid structure had deep technical implications ‚Äî level streaming, seamless transitions between 400m zones, and how biome boundaries are handled. I worked closely with engineers to define level boundaries, connection points, and streaming budgets that were technically feasible while preserving gameplay flow across zone edges. This was heavily R&D-driven ‚Äî many approaches were prototyped and discarded before we found solutions that worked.

With Art

The modular kit was a joint effort with the art team. I defined the gameplay requirements ‚Äî module sizes, snap grid, sight line needs, cover dimensions ‚Äî while artists ensured the modules could receive art passes that matched the Atomic Heart visual identity. Each biome needed distinct visual language while sharing the same modular grammar underneath.

Establishing Shared Metrics

One of the first things I produced was a level design metrics document ‚Äî covering corridor widths, room sizes, sight line distances, cover spacing, and engagement ranges. This became the shared language between all three departments: engineers built streaming around these distances, artists sized props to these dimensions, and designers built levels to these rules.

05

Mentoring Junior Designers

Two of the six-person LD team were juniors. In pre-production, when processes are still being invented, juniors can easily get stuck or produce work that doesn't align with the evolving vision. I invested time in structured onboarding and ongoing mentorship to make them productive fast.

  1. 1 Modular kit as training tool ‚Äî The kit wasn't just a production accelerator; it was a teaching device. Juniors learned level design principles (flow, cover, sight lines) by assembling modules rather than modeling from scratch. The constraints of the kit guided them toward good design decisions.
  2. 2 Metrics document as reference ‚Äî Instead of relying on verbal feedback, juniors had a written spec to check their work against. Corridor too narrow? Check the doc. Engagement range too short? The metrics say why.
  3. 3 Review cadence ‚Äî Regular blockout reviews where I walked through their work, explaining not just what to fix but why ‚Äî connecting spatial decisions to gameplay outcomes.
Result

Both juniors were independently building levels within the first few weeks ‚Äî producing work that met the project's quality bar without constant oversight. The combination of modular kit + metrics doc + regular reviews created a self-reinforcing system that scaled mentoring beyond one-on-one time.

06

Key Takeaways

  1. ‚Üí Pre-production is about building systems, not content. My biggest contribution wasn't any single level ‚Äî it was the modular kit, the metrics framework, and the pipeline that let a 6-person team tackle 54 levels with confidence.
  2. ‚Üí Gameplay trumps realism in metrics. The 4m grid was "wrong" architecturally but right for the game. Prioritizing how spaces play over how they look on a blueprint produced better combat encounters.
  3. ‚Üí Tools are mentorship at scale. A well-designed modular kit teaches level design principles through its constraints. Juniors learn by doing ‚Äî and the kit makes "doing" produce good results from day one.
  4. ‚Üí Cross-department alignment starts in pre-production. The metrics document wasn't just for LD ‚Äî it became the shared vocabulary between engineering, art, and design. Investing in that shared language early prevented months of misalignment later.
