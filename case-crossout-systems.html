Carrying Capacity Rework

01

The Problem

Crossout Mobile is a vehicular combat game where players build custom vehicles from parts ‚Äî cabins, weapons, armor, and movement parts (wheels, hovers, tank tracks). The movement system had a fundamental balance issue that was warping the entire competitive meta.

Core Issue

Movement parts (wheels, hovers, tracks) generated carrying capacity instead of just consuming it. This meant players could stack hovers endlessly ‚Äî each one adding more capacity, allowing heavier weapons and more armor. The system rewarded excess with no diminishing returns. The project manager described it as "Baron Munchausen pulling himself up by his own hair."

This created a cascade of problems: hovers were the clear "best" movement type due to their FPS-like strafing controls combined with unlimited stacking potential. Tank tracks, despite their high individual capacity, were slow and inefficient by comparison. Wheels had no meaningful differentiation between variants. Players had figured out the optimal formula and the build meta had stagnated.

02

Analysis & Root Cause

The project manager initially documented 5 interconnected problems with the movement system. After reviewing the analysis, I identified that they all shared a single root cause: the absence of an upper bound on movement part stacking.

Key Insight

The constructor (vehicle builder) was the core of the game ‚Äî players loved the freedom to build anything. Any solution had to preserve the feeling of freedom while introducing healthy constraints. Removing carrying capacity from movement parts entirely would break the constructor's foundation.

Instead of removing what worked, I needed to add what was missing: a resource limiter that creates meaningful decisions about how many and which movement parts to use, while still allowing movement parts to contribute carrying capacity.

03

The Solution: Wheel Points & Cabin Limits

I designed a new resource model that introduced constraints at the cabin level without dismantling the existing system. Movement parts still provide carrying capacity ‚Äî but now within clearly defined boundaries.

  1. 1 Wheel Points (new resource) ‚Äî Each cabin provides a fixed number of Wheel Points. Every movement part consumes Wheel Points when installed. This directly limits how many wheels/hovers/tracks a vehicle can carry.
  2. 2 Carrying Capacity Cap ‚Äî A maximum carrying capacity is now defined on each cabin. Movement parts still add capacity, but it cannot exceed the cabin's limit. No more infinite stacking.
  3. 3 Critical Mass Cap ‚Äî Similarly capped at the cabin level, preventing unlimited armor stacking through movement part abuse.
  4. 4 Power Bonus for unused Wheel Points ‚Äî If a player uses fewer movement parts than their cabin allows, the unused Wheel Points convert into a power (acceleration) bonus. This creates a genuine trade-off: more wheels = more capacity but slower acceleration; fewer wheels = less capacity but snappier handling.
Design Philosophy

The solution doesn't punish players ‚Äî it creates meaningful choices. Every slot matters. Do you stack hovers for maximum carry weight, or leave some slots empty for a power bonus? Heavy cabin with tracks for armor, or light cabin with wheels for speed? The "right" answer now depends on your build strategy, not a single dominant formula.

What the Data Shows

Hovers now have the lowest carrying capacity across all cabin types ‚Äî they trade capacity for mobility. Wheels and tracks offer significantly more armor budget but at the cost of speed or agility. Medium cabin + Tracks now gets a 25% power bonus from unused Wheel Points, making tracks a viable aggressive option. Each combination has a distinct identity and trade-off profile.

06

Validation on Live Player Data

Theoretical balance is one thing ‚Äî but Crossout Mobile is a constructor game where players build unpredictable combinations. We needed to test these fundamental changes against real player builds from production servers.

  1. 1 Top competitive builds ‚Äî Exported the top 13 PvP and top 13 PvE builds from live servers and ran them through the new system to evaluate impact.
  2. 2 Active player sample ‚Äî Pulled builds from random active players (epic-tier cabins and movement parts, 10+ battles in the past week) to assess impact on the general population.
  3. 3 Impact assessment ‚Äî Checked which builds would break, which would be buffed, and which remained stable. Tuned parameters to minimize disruption to fair builds while correctly nerfing exploitative ones.

This approach ‚Äî combining theoretical modeling with production data validation ‚Äî gave us high confidence that the rework would improve balance without alienating the existing player base.

07

Implementation & Outcome

The rework was implemented in collaboration with the engineering team. The scope included:

  1. 1 New Wheel Points system coded into cabins and movement parts
  2. 2 Carrying capacity and critical mass caps added to all 50+ cabins
  3. 3 Power bonus formula implemented for unused Wheel Points
  4. 4 Engine modules extended with limit bonuses (carry cap, critical mass, wheel points)
  5. 5 Full balance pass across all cabins and all movement parts in the game
Result

The rework introduced a healthier meta where all three movement types had distinct roles and trade-offs. Hovers were no longer the universally dominant choice. Tracks became a viable competitive option. The constructor retained its freedom ‚Äî players still had meaningful build variety ‚Äî but within a balanced framework that rewarded strategic thinking over exploitative stacking.

08

Key Takeaways

  1. ‚Üí Constrain, don't remove. The movement parts still generate carrying capacity ‚Äî I added ceilings and resource costs rather than gutting the core mechanic. Preserving what players loved was essential.
  2. ‚Üí Create trade-offs, not nerfs. The power bonus for unused Wheel Points turned a restriction into a choice. Players aren't losing something ‚Äî they're deciding what to prioritize.
  3. ‚Üí Validate on real data. Theoretical balance spreadsheets are necessary but not sufficient. Testing against production builds from live servers caught edge cases that formulas alone would miss.
  4. ‚Üí Communicate the system. The resource flow diagram was critical for aligning the team ‚Äî engineers, producers, and other designers all needed to understand the new model before implementation began.

09

The Problem

Crossout Mobile had a wide arsenal of weapons ‚Äî cannons, machine guns, shotguns, rockets ‚Äî but within each weapon class, individual weapons played nearly identically. Players had no reason to choose one cannon over another beyond raw power score. The result: no meaningful weapon selection, no build diversity within a class, and a stale combat meta.

Core Issue

Weapons within the same class had similar stats and no distinct gameplay identity. A "choice" between two cannons was really just picking whichever had higher numbers. There was no trade-off, no playstyle differentiation, and no reason to experiment with different weapons of the same type.

Systems Synergy

Weapon choice now influenced the entire vehicle build. A rotating brawler cannon pairs naturally with a heavy cabin + tracks (tank hits, stay in the fight). A fixed sniper cannon works best on a light cabin + hovers (reposition quickly, maintain distance). The weapon rework amplified the carrying capacity rework ‚Äî together, they created a build meta where every component choice mattered.

12

Live Balance Process

After launching the archetype rework, I ran ongoing balance passes every patch, using production analytics to monitor weapon health across different player segments.

  1. 1 Win rate tracking ‚Äî Monitored win rates per weapon across different leagues and player cohorts. Flagged statistically significant deviations from the 50% target.
  2. 2 Popularity analysis ‚Äî Tracked pick rates per weapon per energy cost. Unpopular weapons got stat buffs (damage, durability) before any mechanic changes ‚Äî the simplest fix first.
  3. 3 Detachment rates ‚Äî Monitored what percentage of a weapon's instances get destroyed in combat. Used to tune durability: close-range weapons got higher HP, long-range got lower ‚Äî reinforcing each archetype's intended risk profile.
  4. 4 DPE (Damage Per Energy) ‚Äî Energy is a build resource: each weapon costs energy to equip (e.g., a machine gun costs 2, a cannon costs 4). DPE normalizes damage output by energy cost, ensuring weapons at the same energy investment deal comparable value. Weapons consistently above or below target DPE got damage adjustments.
Balance Philosophy

The goal was never perfect 50% win rate for every weapon ‚Äî it was meaningful choice within healthy bounds. Each weapon should have a scenario where it's the best pick, and no weapon should be the best pick in every scenario. When the data showed a weapon outside acceptable bounds, I adjusted the simplest parameter first (usually damage), then durability, and only changed mechanics as a last resort.

13

Weapon Rework Takeaways

  1. ‚Üí Differentiation through trade-offs, not power. Making weapons feel different isn't about one being "better" ‚Äî it's about each being better at something specific and worse at something else.
  2. ‚Üí Perks amplify identity. Stat differences define archetypes; perks make them memorable. A speed boost on a close-range gun doesn't just adjust numbers ‚Äî it tells the player "this is a rush weapon."
  3. ‚Üí Systems compound. The weapon rework made the carrying capacity rework more meaningful, and vice versa. When individual systems create clear identities, the combinatorial space of the constructor becomes richer.
  4. ‚Üí Simplest fix first. In live-service balance, always try the least invasive change. Buff damage before reworking mechanics. Monitor for a full patch cycle before adjusting again. Data over instinct.
