Design Principles

Crossout Mobile maps needed to support fast-paced vehicular combat on mobile ‚Äî short matches, clear navigation, and fair competition. I established a set of core principles that guided every map I designed:

Core Structure

Every map is built as a network of roads connecting combat arenas. Unlike the PC version (Crossout), we made all maps fully symmetrical (with minor visual variation) to guarantee fair PvP on both sides.

  1. 1 Symmetrical layout ‚Äî Both teams start with identical spatial advantages. Mirrored geometry ensures no side has an inherent win-rate edge.
  2. 2 Key arenas (front lines) ‚Äî Central contact zones where teams meet. These are the primary engagement areas designed for head-on combat with cover variety.
  3. 3 Sniper positions ‚Äî Elevated or distant vantage points with sight lines to key arenas. Reward positioning and ranged builds, but exposed to flanks.
  4. 4 Shortcuts & stealth routes ‚Äî Side passages for flanking and rear attacks. Give aggressive players options to break stalemates and punish static teams.
  5. 5 Objective points ‚Äî Placed to support multiple game modes (Capture, Domination, etc.) without requiring separate map variants.
  6. 6 20‚Äì30 second first contact ‚Äî Fast enough to keep mobile sessions engaging, long enough for teams to choose their approach lane.

Design & Production Process

I owned maps end-to-end ‚Äî from initial concept through production to release. Over time, I formalized this into a repeatable pipeline that the team could follow consistently.

Step 1: Concept & Reference

Each map starts with a theme and reference board ‚Äî real-world locations, mood, and visual identity. The theme drives both the visual language and the gameplay opportunities (e.g., an industrial substation offers vertical structures for sniper nests; a frozen dam creates long sight lines with limited cover).

Reference collection for map concept ‚Äî visual themes, real-world locations, architectural elements.

Step 2: Paper Design

A top-down schematic defining the road network, arena placement, spawn points, sight lines, and flow. This is where the core gameplay decisions happen ‚Äî lane count, symmetry verification, engagement distances, flanking opportunities.

Paper design phase ‚Äî road network, combat arenas, spawn positions, and flow arrows annotated on a top-down schematic.

Step 3: Blockout / Greybox

The first pass in Unity ‚Äî simple geometry to validate scale, flow, and gameplay. At this stage I test drive times, sight lines, cover placement, and whether the map "reads" correctly from the player's perspective. Vehicles in Crossout Mobile vary massively in size and speed, so metrics need to accommodate everything from small buggies to heavy trucks.

Blockout: top-down view showing road layout and arena volumes.
Blockout: player perspective testing scale and sight lines.

Step 4: Playtest & Iterate

Internal playtests with the team to validate flow, pacing, and balance. Key things I look for: do both sides reach the first arena at the same time? Are sniper positions overpowered? Do shortcuts feel rewarding without being mandatory? Feedback is collected, iterated on, and retested until the map feels fair and fun.

Step 5: Art Pass

Final visual production ‚Äî terrain sculpting, props, lighting, composition, and texturing. I handled the full art pass on many maps myself (Substation, Frozen Dam, Sinto City), working on composition, lighting, terrain, and prop modeling in Unity and Blender.

Final art pass ‚Äî in-game environment with lighting and props.
Final art pass ‚Äî different angle showing composition and atmosphere.

Step 6: Ship & Live Iteration

After release, I monitored heatmaps and analytics to identify balance issues ‚Äî areas where fights cluster too heavily, dead zones players avoid, or sight lines that give one position an unfair advantage. Post-launch tuning was an ongoing part of the live-service cycle.

Production Pipeline

When I was promoted to Lead Level Designer, one of my first initiatives was to formalize the map production process into a repeatable pipeline that the whole team could follow. This was critical for hitting the 2-month release cadence with a small team (2 designers + 2 artists).

  1. 1 Concept lock (Week 1) ‚Äî Theme, references, and paper design reviewed and approved by Lead GD and Art Director.
  2. 2 Blockout review (Week 2‚Äì3) ‚Äî Greybox built and playtested. Metrics validated: drive times, sight lines, arena sizes, symmetry.
  3. 3 Gameplay lock (Week 4) ‚Äî Layout finalized after playtest iteration. No major structural changes after this point.
  4. 4 Art production (Week 5‚Äì7) ‚Äî Terrain, props, lighting, composition. Parallel work between designer and artist where possible.
  5. 5 Polish & QA (Week 8) ‚Äî Performance optimization (critical for mobile), bug fixes, final playtest, ship.
Result

This pipeline enabled the team to consistently deliver 6 PvP maps per year ‚Äî one every two months ‚Äî while maintaining quality and gameplay balance. It also freed me up to focus on systems design work (weapon balance, progression, economy) alongside map production.

06

Key Takeaways

  1. ‚Üí Symmetry is non-negotiable for mobile PvP. Players on mobile have shorter sessions and lower tolerance for feeling cheated. Symmetrical layouts removed a whole category of balance complaints.
  2. ‚Üí Design for the vehicle, not the character. Crossout Mobile vehicles range from tiny buggies to massive trucks. Every corridor, cover piece, and arena needed to accommodate this full range of sizes and speeds.
  3. ‚Üí Pipeline enables quality at speed. Formalizing the process from concept to ship meant less ad-hoc decision making and more predictable delivery ‚Äî critical for a live-service team.
  4. ‚Üí Ship, then listen. Heatmaps and analytics after launch revealed things no amount of internal playtesting could catch. Post-launch iteration was as important as the initial design.
